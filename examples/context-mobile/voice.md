# Voice

## Tone
Warm, encouraging, calm, conversational, gently playful

## Writing Principles
- **Talk like a friend, not a brand.** "Nice work on your morning walk!" not "Congratulations on completing your daily wellness activity." Every message should feel like it could come from someone who knows you.
- **Keep it short and kind.** Notifications are 10 words or less. In-app messages are 1-2 sentences. Nobody wants to read a paragraph when they're trying to check off a habit.
- **Celebrate the small wins.** A 3-day streak is worth acknowledging. Completing 4 out of 5 habits is great. The app should feel generous with recognition.
- **Never guilt, always encourage.** "Ready to pick back up?" not "You missed yesterday." Frame everything as forward motion, never as failure.
- **Use second person naturally.** "You" and "your" — direct and personal. Avoid "users" or "one" or passive constructions. The app is talking to one person.

## Platform Variations
- **App Store listing**: Benefit-led, scannable. Lead with the emotional outcome ("Build habits that actually last"), not the feature list. Keywords for ASO baked into natural sentences. Social proof: rating, user count, press mentions.
- **Push notifications**: Ultra-short. Warm. Never needy. Mix of nudges ("Morning walk time?"), celebrations ("7 days strong!"), and reflections ("How did this week feel?"). Personalized with habit name when possible.
- **In-app copy**: Friendly and minimal. Onboarding is conversational ("What's one habit you'd like to start with?"). Empty states are encouraging, not blank. Error messages are human ("Something went wrong on our end. Try again in a sec?").
- **Instagram/TikTok**: Relatable content about habit struggles, not perfection. "Day 4 of remembering to drink water" energy. Behind-the-scenes of building the app. User stories (with permission). Carousel tips on habit science.
- **Email**: Personal, from a real name (not "The Steadily Team"). Weekly digests feel like a check-in, not a report. Unsubscribe is one click, no guilt.
- **Blog**: Longer-form habit science, tips, and user stories. Conversational but backed by research. Links to studies when citing claims. Warm intros, practical takeaways.

## Examples

### Example 1 (Push notification — nudge)
> Time for your evening journal? You've been on a 12-day streak.

### Example 2 (Push notification — missed day)
> No worries about yesterday. Your streak shield kept things safe. Ready for today?

### Example 3 (App Store description opening)
> You don't need another habit app with 50 features you'll never use. You need one that helps you actually stick with it. Steadily keeps it simple: pick your habits, tap to complete, and build real momentum with streaks, gentle nudges, and weekly reflections.

### Example 4 (In-app empty state — first habit)
> Let's start with just one. What's a small habit you'd like to do every day? Don't overthink it — you can always change it later.

### Example 5 (Weekly reflection prompt)
> Quick check-in: How did this week go? What felt easy, what felt hard, and is there anything you want to adjust? Take a minute — it helps more than you'd think.

### Example 6 (Instagram caption)
> Week 3 of building this app and my own streak broke on day 6. Used a streak shield. Felt surprisingly okay about it. Maybe that's the point — it's not about being perfect, it's about keeping going.

## Words We Use
- **habit** (instead of "task" or "goal" — habits are daily, goals are distant)
- **streak** (instead of "chain" or "sequence")
- **check in** (instead of "log" or "record")
- **pick back up** (instead of "restart" or "try again" — no implication of failure)
- **gentle nudge** (instead of "reminder" or "alert")
- **reflection** (instead of "review" or "analysis")

## Words We Never Use
- **failure** — ever, in any context
- **discipline** — feels punishing; use "consistency" or "routine"
- **grind** — hustle culture language
- **optimize** — clinical and cold; we're building habits, not running A/B tests on people
- **users** — in public-facing copy; use "you" or "people"
- **Don't forget!** — anxiety-inducing; nudge, don't nag

## Signature Patterns
- Push notifications end with the habit name or streak number when possible
- Celebration messages use exclamation marks sparingly — one per message max
- Weekly reflections always ask a question, never give a lecture
- Social media posts often start with a personal, relatable moment
- Emails sign off with just a first name (e.g., "— Maya")
